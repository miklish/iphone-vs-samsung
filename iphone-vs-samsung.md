# iPhone vs Samsung (OneUI)

----

After seeing the complete and utter beatdown macOS provides over Windows (its not even a fair fight -- Windows is a horrific dumpster fire of an OS compared to macOS. An invasive vehicle to sell you garbage and push MS garbage-products on you), I have to say I was disapointed by iOS.

## iPhone minuses
----
- no useful notification previews - huge
  - no way to preview app notification on the app's icon when it has a notification badge
  - no way to do so in notification pull down (too little info)
  - no notification history
  - no simple expand / retract controls on notifications
    - have to tap outside area to dismiss
    - sometimes trigger things you do not want
  - constantly presented with 30 - 50 notification all in a giant list
- no side slide-in Edge Panel
  - Samsung Edge Panel is accessible at all times -- even if in another app
  - I extensively used this for:
    - calling frequent contacts
    - launching common apps like:
      - 2FA Auth apps
      - email/notes/ChatGPT/calendar/calculator etc...
- not a one handed OS - need to hit top of screen all the time
- widgets are very restrictive
  - limited sizes
  - no functionality in widget - just info and a link
- iPhone Notes is not as easy to use as Samsung Notes
- no "swipe down anywhere on homescreen" for notifications
  - always have to reach (again) up to the top
- bad Google integration
  - no always-on Google assistant
- no split screen
- no access to the Settings app from Control Center!
  - must track down Settings app icon on homescreen
- app settings are often not accessible from in the app!
  - must
    - 1. track down Settings app icon on homescreen
    - 2. go to `Apps`
    - 3. search for the app
    - 4. edit settings from there
- no access to an app's settings directly from it's icon (via long press)
- no universal back button!!!
  - every app may have a different method to go back!
  - constantly (again!) have to reach to the top of the phone to hit a "back" icon
    - unless the app supports the back gesture -- but you never know which do and which don't
  - no ability to universaly move backwards through nested apps
  - iPhone has this teeny link you need to click to go backwards through the chain of open apps
    - e.g.: If app A launches app B which launces app C (app chain)
  - no natural language settings search
    - need to know the precise setting name!
  - FaceID is -- overall -- not as good as through-screen fingerprint unlock
    - While I appreciate the total coolness and tech behind FaceID, in practical situations, a fingerpring is just simpler and the better choice in the majority of realworld situations
    - I find myself VERY often having to tilt my iPhone sitting on a desk
  - app icons are ludicrously, childishly large with no way to shrink them

## Tie
- Android vs iPhone Keyboard
  - I find the iPhone keyboard to be better than even Gboard at correct predictive text
  - In this case I've found it really "just works"
  - However, on Samsung's keyboard, word correction is simpler since it gives you haptic pulses as you move the cursor through letters and it 'jumps' discretely from one letter to the next so you don't need nearly as much precision to place it at the correct spot in text
  - no button to hide the keyboard. again -- must try and tap somewhere else on the screen and hope it goes away and you don't activate something on screen you didn't want to

## iPhone plusses
----
- consistent smoothness / lack of microstutters and judder
  - as an fps counting / pixel-peeping geek I appreciate the the extreme level of refinement of iOS. every interaction has been painstackingly thought out in how it starts, moves, and resolves. its that good
- ui continuity
  - camera & video
    - looks better on the iPhone - bar none
    - really looks cinematic with no "jelly" blurring
  - I'm the one person that loves the Liquid Glass UI lol
    - Maybe because my first experience with it was iOS 26.2 after they'd fixed many of initial issues
  - Battery during idle
    - The iPhone sips battery if you're not doing anything
      whereas my Samsung still drains battery when idle
    - overnight it often does not drop even 1%
- TruTone - soft on the eyes
    - going back to the Samsung can be a shock to the retinas with its colder sterile feel versus the appropriately warm iPhone screen colours
  - PiP - ability to dock PiP video is very useful (though Samsung may have stolen that idea now)
  - Apps with video or voice features appear like your phone app - giving a very uniform experience for voice across app (like Signal, Whatsapp etc...)
  - Simple, intuitive, almost zero-setup integration with macBooks
    - WiFi hotpost for laptop
    - Passkey sharing <-- huge!
    - Password sharing
    - Find My Phone / Mac
    - data transfer between devices
    - phone screen mirroring on desktop
  - Dynamic island is useful as a way to switch from the foreground app to another but easily see the status of background apps (like phone calls for example)
  - spatial images are very cool
  - heavily rounded corners cause issues in games and websites

## Summary
Despite these plusses, the iPhone is still more cumbersome to use on a daily basis in common everyday tasks.
Tasks that take one or two clicks one-handed on the Samsung typlically take far more clicks, searching, and using both hands to accomplish on the iPhone

And yes, its clear that Samsung steals tons of ideas from iOS, but -- in my use -- the Samsung versions of these features is just better implemented with a focus on efficiency of actions and one-handed useability
